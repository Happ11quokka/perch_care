# Thermal Burns in Parrots

## Overview

Thermal burns are tissue injuries caused by contact with hot liquids, hot surfaces, or steam. Parrots are exceptionally vulnerable to thermal burns because avian skin is significantly thinner than mammalian skin, lacking the thick dermal layer that provides some degree of thermal protection in dogs and cats. A parrot's skin can sustain a full-thickness burn in seconds of contact with a hot surface or liquid that would only cause a superficial burn in a mammal. Common scenarios include birds landing on hot cookware, falling into pots of hot soup or water, walking across active stovetops, and contact with radiators, irons, or curling irons. Kitchen accidents are the leading cause of thermal burns in companion parrots and are largely preventable.

## Affected Species

All parrot species are susceptible to thermal burns. Free-flighted birds are at highest risk because they can land unexpectedly on hot surfaces such as stoves, frying pans, or pots of boiling water. Smaller species including budgerigars, cockatiels, and lovebirds may sustain proportionally more severe injuries due to their small body mass and extremely thin skin. Larger parrots such as macaws and cockatoos, while more robust, frequently sustain foot and keel burns from landing on hot cookware. Curious juvenile birds exploring the household environment are particularly at risk.

## Causes

- **Hot liquid scalds**: Falling into or being splashed by hot soup, boiling water, coffee, tea, or cooking oil
- **Hot surface contact**: Landing on active stove burners, hot frying pans, pots, baking sheets, or oven doors
- **Household appliances**: Contact with radiators, space heaters, clothes irons, curling irons, hair straighteners, or heat lamps positioned too close to the cage
- **Steam burns**: Exposure to steam from boiling pots, kettles, pressure cookers, or steam irons
- **Hot food or beverages**: Birds dipping into or being accidentally splashed with hot human food or drinks
- **Candle and open flame contact**: Landing near or flying over lit candles or gas stove flames

## Symptoms

**Burn severity grading:**

- **First-degree burns (superficial)**: Redness and mild swelling of the skin; feather loss in the affected area; bird may show mild discomfort and increased preening of the burned area
- **Second-degree burns (partial thickness)**: Blistering, significant swelling, and weeping of fluid from the burned area; visible skin erosion; feather loss; moderate to severe pain; reluctance to use affected limb
- **Third-degree burns (full thickness)**: Charred, white, or leathery skin; tissue destruction extending through the full dermis; paradoxically less painful at the burn center due to nerve destruction; surrounding tissue remains extremely painful; potential exposure of underlying muscle or bone

**General signs:**
- Vocalization and agitation immediately after the burn event
- Reluctance to perch or bear weight on burned feet
- Holding wings away from the body if wing or body burns
- Decreased appetite and lethargy
- Fluffed feathers and signs of shock in severe cases
- Secondary infection developing over 24-72 hours: swelling, discharge, and foul odor

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis is based on the witnessed or suspected burn event and characteristic appearance of the affected tissue. Physical examination assesses the location, depth, and total body surface area involved. Burns affecting more than 10% of body surface area are considered critical in birds. Bacterial culture and sensitivity testing of burn wounds guide antibiotic selection when secondary infection is suspected. Complete blood count and blood chemistry panels assess systemic inflammatory response and organ function. Serial examination is necessary because the full extent of thermal tissue damage may not become apparent for 24-48 hours after the initial injury.

## Treatment

**Immediate first aid:**
1. Remove the bird from the heat source immediately
2. Gently run cool (not cold, not ice) water over the burned area for 5-10 minutes to stop the progression of thermal damage; do NOT apply ice, ice water, butter, or ointments
3. Do not attempt to remove adhered feathers or skin from the burn
4. Keep the bird warm overall to prevent hypothermia while cooling only the burn area
5. Place the bird in a quiet, warm, dark transport container
6. Transport to an avian veterinarian immediately

**Veterinary treatment:**
- **Wound care**: Gentle cleaning with sterile saline, debridement of necrotic tissue, and application of topical antimicrobial agents such as silver sulfadiazine cream
- **Pain management**: Meloxicam for anti-inflammatory analgesia; butorphanol or tramadol for moderate to severe pain
- **Antibiotics**: Systemic broad-spectrum antibiotics to prevent or treat secondary infection of burn wounds, guided by culture and sensitivity when available
- **Fluid therapy**: IV or subcutaneous fluids to maintain hydration and support circulation, as fluid loss through burned tissue can be substantial
- **Nutritional support**: Gavage feeding for birds unable to eat due to pain, lethargy, or burns affecting the beak or oral cavity
- **Bandaging**: Non-adherent dressings changed regularly to protect burn wounds and maintain a moist healing environment
- **Serial monitoring**: Repeated wound assessments over days to weeks, as burn injuries evolve and may require ongoing debridement

## Prevention

- **Never allow birds in the kitchen while cooking**: This is the single most important preventive measure; kitchen accidents are the leading cause of thermal burns in parrots
- **Keep birds caged during meal preparation**: Even birds that are well-trained can be startled and fly unpredictably toward hot surfaces
- **Cover hot beverages and food**: Use lids on cups and bowls when birds are out of their cage
- **Use caution with space heaters and radiators**: Place protective barriers around heat sources or keep birds away from rooms with active heaters
- **Secure household appliances**: Unplug and store irons, curling irons, and hair straighteners when not in use
- **Never position heat lamps too close to cages**: Follow manufacturer guidelines for minimum distance from the cage
- **Avoid candles in bird-accessible areas**: Use LED candles as a safer alternative

## Prognosis

Prognosis depends on the severity and extent of the burn. First-degree burns affecting a small area have a good prognosis and typically heal within one to two weeks with appropriate care. Second-degree burns carry a fair to good prognosis if treated promptly, though healing may take several weeks and may result in scarring and permanent feather loss in the affected area. Third-degree burns and burns covering a large body surface area carry a guarded to poor prognosis due to the risk of overwhelming infection, fluid loss, and systemic organ damage. Burns to the feet can result in permanent loss of gripping ability. Early and aggressive veterinary intervention significantly improves outcomes.
