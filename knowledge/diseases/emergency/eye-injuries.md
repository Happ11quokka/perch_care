# Eye Injuries in Parrots

## Overview

Eye injuries in parrots encompass a range of conditions including corneal scratches and ulcers, blunt trauma to the periorbital area, chemical and thermal damage, foreign body penetration, and injuries inflicted by other birds or household pets. Avian eyes are proportionally large relative to skull size and are positioned laterally, providing wide visual coverage but also making them vulnerable to trauma. The avian eye has unique anatomical features including a pecten oculi (a vascular structure within the vitreous body) and a scleral ossicle ring, both of which can be damaged by trauma. Eye injuries are always considered urgent in avian medicine because rapid progression of infection or inflammation can lead to permanent vision loss within hours to days.

## Affected Species

All parrot species are susceptible to eye injuries. Species housed in multi-bird environments, such as budgerigars, cockatiels, and lovebirds, are at increased risk of eye injuries from cage mates during territorial disputes. Larger parrots with prominent periorbital skin, such as macaws with their bare facial patches, may be more vulnerable to periorbital trauma and chemical splash. Free-flighted birds are at risk of corneal scratches from collisions with objects. Birds housed near kitchens are vulnerable to steam and chemical fume exposure. Species that are prey targets, especially smaller parrots, are at higher risk when household cats or dogs are present.

## Causes

- **Trauma from other birds**: Beak bites during aggressive encounters targeting the face and eyes; toe scratches from cage mates
- **Cat scratches and bites**: Cat claws carry bacteria that cause rapid and severe eye infections; even a glancing scratch across the eye can result in corneal perforation
- **Foreign bodies**: Seed husks, feather fragments, cage substrate particles, or dust lodging under the eyelid or on the corneal surface
- **Chemical splash**: Household cleaning products, aerosol sprays, perfumes, or cooking fume exposure irritating or damaging the cornea and conjunctiva
- **Thermal injury**: Steam burns near the eyes, proximity to heat sources causing corneal desiccation
- **Blunt trauma**: Collision with windows, walls, or furniture during flight; being struck by objects; being stepped on
- **Self-inflicted trauma**: Excessive rubbing or scratching at the eyes due to underlying irritation or infection
- **Sharp objects in the environment**: Exposed wire ends on cages, splintered wooden toys, or protruding hardware

## Symptoms

- **Squinting or holding the eye closed (blepharospasm)**: One of the earliest and most reliable indicators of eye pain
- **Ocular discharge**: Watery, mucoid, or purulent discharge from the affected eye
- **Periorbital swelling**: Puffiness around the eye, ranging from mild to severe
- **Redness of the conjunctiva**: Visible hyperemia of the conjunctival membranes
- **Rubbing the eye**: Rubbing the affected eye against the perch, cage bars, or wing
- **Cloudiness or opacity of the cornea**: Indicating corneal edema, ulceration, or abscess
- **Visible wound or blood**: Laceration of the eyelid, hemorrhage within or around the eye
- **Behavioral changes**: Head tilting, reluctance to fly, loss of depth perception, bumping into objects on the affected side
- **Decreased appetite**: Due to pain and stress
- **Photophobia**: Avoiding bright light, seeking darker areas of the cage

## Diagnosis

A thorough ophthalmic examination is essential and should be performed by an avian veterinarian experienced in avian ophthalmology. **Fluorescein staining** is the primary diagnostic test for corneal ulcers: fluorescein dye is applied to the corneal surface and adheres to areas where the epithelium is damaged, fluorescing bright green under cobalt blue or Wood's lamp illumination. **Direct and indirect ophthalmoscopy** evaluates the anterior and posterior chambers, lens, and retinal structures. **Slit-lamp biomicroscopy**, when available, provides detailed assessment of corneal depth and anterior chamber structures. **Tonometry** measures intraocular pressure to detect glaucoma secondary to trauma or uveitis. **Bacterial culture and sensitivity** of ocular discharge guides appropriate antibiotic selection. **Radiographs or CT imaging** may be indicated for periorbital fractures or suspected retrobulbar abscess. Complete blood count and chemistry panel assess systemic inflammatory response, especially if infection is suspected.

## Treatment

**Immediate first aid:**
1. Prevent the bird from rubbing or scratching the affected eye; an Elizabethan collar may be needed
2. If chemical exposure occurred, gently flush the eye with sterile saline or clean lukewarm water for several minutes
3. Do not attempt to remove embedded foreign bodies
4. Do not apply any medications without veterinary guidance, as some eye drops (especially those containing steroids) can worsen corneal ulcers
5. Keep the bird in a quiet, dimly lit environment to reduce photophobia and stress
6. Transport to an avian veterinarian immediately

**Veterinary treatment:**
- **Corneal ulcer management**: Topical antibiotic eye drops (such as ciprofloxacin, ofloxacin, or chloramphenicol) applied multiple times daily; topical atropine for pain relief and to prevent synechia; avoidance of topical corticosteroids until ulcer is fully healed
- **Foreign body removal**: Removal under magnification with topical anesthesia, followed by antibiotic therapy
- **Periorbital wound care**: Cleaning, debridement, and suturing of eyelid lacerations
- **Systemic antibiotics**: For deep infections, periorbital cellulitis, or cat-inflicted wounds where Pasteurella infection is a concern
- **Pain management**: Meloxicam for anti-inflammatory analgesia; butorphanol for moderate to severe pain
- **Elizabethan collar**: To prevent self-trauma during healing
- **Surgical intervention**: Indicated for corneal perforation (corneal grafting), lens luxation, retrobulbar abscess drainage, or enucleation (eye removal) when the eye is non-salvageable and causing ongoing pain
- **Follow-up fluorescein staining**: To monitor corneal ulcer healing; repeat staining every 3-7 days until the ulcer is confirmed healed

## Prevention

- **Supervise interactions between birds**: Separate aggressive individuals; provide adequate cage space to reduce territorial disputes
- **Keep cats and dogs physically separated from birds at all times**: Cat scratches are a leading cause of severe avian eye injuries
- **Bird-proof the flight area**: Remove sharp objects, cover mirrors and windows with decals, and pad hard surfaces
- **Avoid aerosolized chemicals near birds**: Do not use sprays, harsh cleaning products, or perfumes in rooms where birds are housed
- **Inspect cages regularly**: Check for protruding wire ends, broken cage bars, or sharp hardware that could injure eyes
- **Ensure appropriate toy selection**: Avoid toys with sharp edges, splintering wood, or small parts that could break and become projectiles
- **Maintain clean cage environments**: Reduce dust and particulate matter that can irritate eyes
- **Position cages away from kitchens**: Prevent steam and cooking fume exposure

## Prognosis

Prognosis depends on the type and severity of the eye injury. Superficial corneal scratches and small uncomplicated ulcers generally heal well within 5-14 days with appropriate topical antibiotic therapy, carrying a good prognosis for full vision recovery. Deep corneal ulcers, descemetoceles, and corneal perforations carry a guarded prognosis and may result in partial or complete vision loss even with aggressive treatment. Cat-inflicted eye injuries carry a guarded prognosis due to the virulence of Pasteurella and other feline oral and claw flora. Blunt trauma causing hyphema (blood in the anterior chamber), lens luxation, or retinal detachment carries a poor prognosis for vision restoration. Enucleation, while resulting in permanent monocular vision, is well-tolerated by parrots and allows a good quality of life. Early veterinary intervention is the most important factor in preserving vision.
