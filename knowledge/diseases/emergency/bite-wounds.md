# Bite Wounds in Parrots

## Overview

Bite wounds from other animals are among the most dangerous emergencies in avian medicine. Cat bites pose the greatest threat due to Pasteurella multocida, a gram-negative bacterium that is part of the normal oral flora of virtually all domestic cats. Pasteurella causes fulminant septicemia in birds, and without aggressive antibiotic treatment initiated within hours of the bite, the infection can progress to fatal sepsis within 24-72 hours, even from a single seemingly minor puncture wound. Dog bites cause severe crushing and tearing injuries that can result in internal organ damage, fractures, and hemorrhage. Bites from other birds can cause significant soft tissue damage, especially to feet, toes, and the face. Rodent bites, while less common, introduce their own spectrum of bacterial pathogens. Any bite wound in a parrot should be treated as a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate veterinary care.

## Affected Species

All parrot species are vulnerable to bite wounds. Smaller species such as budgerigars, cockatiels, lovebirds, and parrotlets are at the highest risk of fatal outcomes because a single cat bite can inflict proportionally devastating damage to their small bodies, and the rapid bacterial dissemination in a small bird can outpace treatment. Medium-sized parrots such as conures, caiques, and Senegal parrots are also highly vulnerable. Larger species such as macaws, cockatoos, and Amazon parrots may survive the initial physical trauma more readily but remain equally susceptible to Pasteurella septicemia from cat bites. Birds with clipped wings or limited flight ability are at increased risk because they cannot escape predatory attacks.

## Causes

- **Cat bites and scratches**: The most dangerous type of bite wound in avian medicine; cat teeth are thin and needle-like, creating deep puncture wounds that seal over quickly, trapping Pasteurella multocida and other bacteria beneath the skin in an anaerobic environment ideal for bacterial proliferation
- **Dog bites**: Cause crushing, tearing, and shaking injuries; the force of a dog bite can fracture bones, rupture air sacs, and cause severe internal hemorrhage; even a playful mouthing by a dog can be fatal to a small bird
- **Other bird bites**: Territorial aggression between cage mates or during introductions; larger parrots can amputate toes and inflict deep beak wounds on smaller birds; breeding aggression and mate-directed violence
- **Rat and rodent bites**: Rats that enter aviaries at night can inflict multiple bite wounds on sleeping birds, particularly targeting feet, toes, and the face; rodent-borne bacteria and potential for rabies transmission in endemic areas
- **Ferret attacks**: Ferrets are predators with strong prey drive toward birds and can inflict rapid, lethal bite injuries

## Symptoms

**Immediate signs:**
- Visible puncture wounds, lacerations, or torn tissue
- Bleeding from wound sites; note that cat puncture wounds may appear deceptively minor with minimal external bleeding
- Vocalization and extreme agitation immediately after the attack
- Feather loss and damage at the bite site
- Difficulty breathing if the chest wall or air sacs are punctured

**Signs of developing infection (6-72 hours after cat bite):**
- Progressive lethargy and fluffed feathers
- Loss of appetite and cessation of drinking
- Sitting on the cage floor rather than perching
- Swelling, redness, and heat at the wound site
- Purulent or foul-smelling discharge from wounds
- Fever (difficult to assess clinically in birds, but manifested by lethargy and panting)
- Signs of septic shock: cold feet, unresponsiveness, rapid breathing, collapse

**Late-stage septicemia (often fatal):**
- Severe depression and recumbency
- Rapid or labored breathing
- Organ failure signs: discolored droppings, cessation of droppings
- Death, often within 24-72 hours of the initial cat bite if untreated

## Diagnosis

Diagnosis is based on the witnessed or suspected attack and physical examination findings. All wounds are carefully examined for depth, number, and proximity to vital structures. Cat puncture wounds require particular attention because the entry point may be tiny while the deep tissue contamination is extensive. Bacterial culture and sensitivity testing of wound samples should be performed but treatment must not be delayed while awaiting results. Complete blood count reveals leukocytosis or leukopenia depending on the stage of infection. Blood chemistry panel assesses organ function. Radiographs evaluate for fractures, air sac rupture, and subcutaneous emphysema. Serial blood work monitors the progression or resolution of infection over the treatment period.

## Treatment

**Immediate first aid:**
1. Control any active bleeding with gentle direct pressure using clean gauze
2. Do not attempt to flush or probe puncture wounds, as this may worsen tissue damage
3. Keep the bird warm, calm, and quiet in a dark, enclosed transport container
4. Transport to an avian veterinarian as an absolute emergency; for cat bites, time to antibiotic administration is the critical factor determining survival
5. If veterinary care is more than one hour away, contact the veterinary clinic by phone for guidance on emergency measures

**Veterinary treatment:**
- **Aggressive antibiotic therapy (critical for cat bites)**: Broad-spectrum antibiotics effective against Pasteurella multocida must be initiated immediately; first-line choices include amoxicillin-clavulanate, fluoroquinolones (enrofloxacin or marbofloxacin), or doxycycline; combination therapy is often warranted for severe infections
- **Wound care**: Thorough wound exploration, gentle irrigation with sterile saline under pressure to flush deep puncture tracts, and debridement of devitalized tissue; cat bite puncture wounds are typically left open to drain rather than sutured closed
- **Pain management**: Meloxicam for anti-inflammatory analgesia; butorphanol or tramadol for moderate to severe pain
- **Fluid therapy**: IV or subcutaneous fluids to support circulation and maintain hydration, particularly critical in septic or shocked patients
- **Fracture stabilization**: Splinting, bandaging, or surgical repair of broken bones resulting from crushing bite injuries
- **Air sac repair**: Management of air sac rupture through rest, antibiotics, and occasionally surgical intervention
- **Nutritional support**: Gavage feeding for birds too debilitated to eat voluntarily
- **Serial monitoring**: Repeated physical examinations, blood work, and wound assessments over a minimum of 7-14 days; Pasteurella infection can recrudesce if antibiotics are discontinued too early

## Prevention

- **Absolute physical separation from cats**: Cats must never have unsupervised access to birds, even through cage bars; a cat can hook a bird through cage bars with a single claw strike; cat-proofing must assume the cat will find access if any opportunity exists
- **Absolute physical separation from dogs**: Dogs should not be left alone with birds regardless of the dog's temperament; predatory instincts can be triggered unpredictably
- **Secure cage and aviary construction**: Use cages with bar spacing too narrow for cat paws or rodent entry; outdoor aviaries must have double-wired walls and secure locks
- **Rodent control**: Eliminate rodent access to aviaries and bird rooms; seal entry points and use bird-safe pest control methods
- **Supervise all inter-species interactions**: Never leave birds with other animals even briefly while answering a door or phone
- **Separate aggressive birds**: House incompatible birds in separate cages; provide visual barriers during introduction periods
- **Nighttime security**: Ensure cages are secured and rooms are closed to prevent nocturnal predator access; rodents and cats are most active at night
- **Educate household members**: All family members and visitors must understand the rules for keeping birds safe from other pets

## Prognosis

Prognosis varies dramatically depending on the type of attacking animal and the speed of veterinary intervention. **Cat bite wounds** carry a guarded to poor prognosis; Pasteurella multocida septicemia can be fatal within 24-72 hours even from a single small puncture. Birds that receive appropriate antibiotics within 1-2 hours of a cat bite have a significantly better survival rate, but delayed treatment beyond 4-6 hours greatly worsens the prognosis. Even with treatment, mortality from cat bites in small parrots remains high. **Dog bite wounds** carry a variable prognosis depending on the severity of physical trauma; minor mouthing injuries may respond well to treatment, while crushing injuries with internal organ damage are often fatal. **Bird bite wounds** generally carry a fair to good prognosis for tissue injuries, though amputated digits and severe beak damage may be permanent. **Rodent bite wounds** carry a fair prognosis with prompt antibiotic treatment. In all cases, immediate veterinary care is the most important determinant of survival.
